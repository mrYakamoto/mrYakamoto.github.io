Regular Expressions

Regular Expressions, often abbreviated as regex, are a frequently used method for defining a search pattern. They're used in many programming languages including Ruby and JavaScript. A regular expression is formed by using patterns of characters in between two forward slashes / /.

Regex are commonly used in methods such as...
search, replace, match, split, test, and exec.

/a/ would relate to the first 'a' in a string, whereas /abc/ would relate to the first 'abc' all together in a string. The | character can be used as an or. For example /a|b|c/ would relate to the first 'a' or 'b' or 'c' character.

brackets are a very commonly used tool of regular expressions. any single character inside brackets will be searched for. So, /[abc]/ would be the same as /a|b|c/. Brackets are also used to search for characters within a range. /[0-5]/ would relate to number characters 0 through 5. /[d-g]/ would relate to letter characters 'd' through 'g'.

Regular expressions also come with 'flags' that can be powerful search tools. 'g' and 'i' are perhaps the most common. They're placed outside the forward slashes like so, /harry/g. 'g' stands for global and will relate to not just the first instance of 'harry' but all instances of it. 'i' will perform a case-insensitive search. /harry/gi will relate to not just all instances of 'harry' but 'Harry', 'hArRY', or any other combination of the same characters without regard to case.

These are just some important basics with regards to regular expressions. Although it's simple to understand their basic use, their usage can become very complicated. For example...
^\(*\d{3}\)*( |-)*\d{3}( |-)*\d{4}$
